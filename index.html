Parametrizing a CDF like a French Automotive Engineer from the 60s

Describe a probability distribution

Every distribution can be defined by a CDF

There are a few constraints to the CDF $F(X)$:

  • Monotone and non-decreasing
  • $0 \leq F(x) \leq 1 \quad \forall x \in \text{domain}(X)$
  • $\lim_{x \to -\infty} F(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to +\infty} F(x) = 1$

Definition of a Bézier curve

A Bézier curve of degree $n$ is defined by $n+1$ control points $P_i$ in normalized coordinates $t$ ("time"): $$B_n(t) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \binom{n}{i} t^i (1-t)^{n-i} P_i, \quad \text{with} \quad t \in [0, 1]$$

Example for Bézier curves of degree 1 to 3

The Bézier curve is named after French engineer Pierre Bézier, who used it in the 1960s for designing curves for the bodywork of Renault cars.

Bézier curves are weighted combinations of Bernstein polynomials

A Bernstein polynomial of degree $n$ is defined as:

$$b_{i,n}(t) = \binom{n}{i} t^i (1-t)^{n-i}, \quad \text{with} \quad i = 0, \dots, n$$

Compare this to the definitiion of the Bézier curve:

$$B_n(t) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \binom{n}{i} t^i (1-t)^{n-i} P_i, \quad \text{with} \quad t \in [0, 1]$$

Bernstein polynomials

$$b_{i,n}(t) = \binom{n}{i} t^i (1-t)^{n-i}, \quad \text{with} \quad i = 0, \dots, n$$

Interesting properties of Bernstein polynomials:

  • They are non-negative: $b_{i,n}(t) \geq 0$ for all $t$ and $i$.
  • They sum to one: $\sum_{i=0}^{n} b_{i,n}(t) = 1$ for all $t$.

Bézier/Bernstein parametrization of the CDF

  1. Bound the domain of $X$ to $[L, U] \rightarrow t \in [0, 1]$
  2. Use a model's output $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$ as logits
    • The dimensionality of the output determines the degree of the Bézier curve $n$
    • Normalize output $y$ $\rightarrow$ factor for local probability mass $\Delta_i$ with $\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \Delta_i = 1$ (e.g., via softmax)
    • The weighting factor from the Bernstein polynomials $b_{i,n}(t)$ is the cumulative sum $\beta_k = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \Delta_i$

  3. Compute the CDF as a weighted sum $$ B(t) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \beta_i b_{i, n}(t), \quad \text{with} \quad \beta_i \ge 0$$

    The $\beta_i$ are the y-coordinates of the CDF's control points. Along the $X$-axis, the control points are uniformly distributed at $t_i = \frac{i}{n}$.

Properties of this Parametrization

  • Valid by construction: the CDF is a convex combination of Bernstein polynomials
  • Unconstrained optimization: useable any deep learning framework
  • Efficient implementation: no numerical integration required since we are parametrizing a CDF
  • Flexible CDF: model output dimension determines the flexibility of the CDF, but $n>150$ causes numerical instability
  • Slow error rate: the worst-case pointwise approximation error converges at $O(1/n)$
  • Peak sharpness: the standard deviation of the sharpest representable peak scales at $O(1/\sqrt{n})$.
